PROJECTIONS

10 January - 22 April 2015

Projections is a series of collaborative experiments in the possibilities and limitations of public art practice in Karachi. Using luminous imagery, we created ephemeral events/moments in various public spaces – commercial markets, intimate residential areas, defunct cinemas, under construction development sites and spaces in transition – in an attempt to imagine and project ourselves, our histories and futures onto the city's surfaces.

Through these intersecting projections we explore how to momentarily inhabit each other’s imagination-each other’s city. The projections rendered familiar spaces uncanny, triggering new ways of viewing, understanding and inhabiting the city. Working within the precarities of public spaces, the projections engaged diverse publics which simultaneously questioned and unsettled our own positionalities.

Collaborating with artists whose research-based practices emerge from the city, these projections took the form of urban transference, performance of desires, evocation of memories, political interventions and engagements with architectural forms, spatial configurations and experiential modes.

  • Funland - Karachi Series

    Bani Abidi

    10 January 2015

    Funland-Karachi Series -Bani Abidi

    The 6 videos in this work meander between being memorials to a cosmopolitan city that fights for its survival to melancholic imaginings of a hypothetical, deserted future. The Karachi iteration of 'Funland' around the streets of the historic Saddar Bazaar, is a unique one, as the work engages physically and literally with the very nerve that runs through the entire work.

    Bani Abidi projected selected videos from her 2014 installation 'Funland' in different locations of Saddar Bazaar, Karachi. 'Funland' is a multichannel video installation which has been produced with the support of the 8th Berlin Biennale and the DAAD Kunstler Programme.

    Projection Sites:

    • Cinema - Opposite Atrium Mall Entrance
    • Funland - Opposite Iceberg Icecream
    • Beach - Price Cinema

  • Whose Mark is it Anyway?

    Shaheen Jaffrani

    17 January 2015

    Whose Mark is it Anyway? - Shaheen Jaffrani

    “The role of urban decay has had a strong influence on my research. Living in a city like Karachi, we are surrounded by marks, smears and erosion everywhere. These subconsciously formed visuals are part of a vicious cycle that contribute towards the bigger picture of our city. Overtime, people have tried to get rid of these marks, fix them…but in my videos and images I work with their appearance and disappearance, commenting on the endlessness of the process.”

    Projection Site : Shafqat House, Deen Muhamms Wafai Road. Near National Museum.

  • Ratan Talao

    Shahana Rajani

    31 January 2015

    Ratan Talao - Shahana Rajani

    Growing up in Karachi – the city of migrants – we have heard many stories of madness and mayhem that define our memories of Partition. In these stories, violence is always imagined elsewhere. Karachi is simply the ever-accommodating city that welcomes muhajirs with open arms. In the ruins of memory is a performance that takes place in Ratan Talao, a muhajir neighbourhood of Karachi. It returns to 1947 to reinsert memories and histories of violence and uprooting that lay buried at the heart of the neighbourhood.

    Projection Site : Ratan Talao

  • Near and far sights / sites converge

    Naiza Khan

    7 February 2015

    Near and far sights / sites converge - Naiza Khan

    Near and far sights/ sites converge was shot through a homemade telescope at Manora beach on a monsoon afternoon in 2014. A circular space was glimpsed, a war ship from the Pakistan Navy fleet, figures amongst the waves in abandonment and freedom. This sense of intimacy and freedom in a public space is shrinking, like the sea itself.

    Manora is a restful anchor for the turbulence of Karachi, a space for non-elite leisure. Crossing the harbour on public taxi boats, visitors come to experience the expanse of horizon, a sight they often miss within the dense, congested spaces of the inner city.

    Working simultaneously in two disparate yet intrinsically connected landscapes within the city of Karachi gives me a sense of the malleability of the urban space. Projecting this video within the dense labyrinth of galis in Golimar was like an inversion of both spaces/spheres. It was like reaching through the lens of the doorbeen, pulling the ocean inwards, and bringing the horizon line into the dense mohalla.

    Projection Site : Golimaar

  • Samundar Ki Chori

    Zambeel Dramatic Readings

    9 Feb 2015

    Samundar Ki Chori -Zambeel Dramatic Readings

    A city next to the sea awakens to a strange phenomenon.

    Asif Farrukhi’s story Samundar ki Chori addresses a very tangible ecological concern in a subtle and unassuming manner, highlighting the very complex psyche and fabric of Karachi. The text opens with the discovery by citizens of a city that the sea has disappeared. The realisation gradually shapes the conviction that it has, in fact, been stolen. Conspiracy theories abound and many assumptions come into play as citizens try to deduce the crime. The usual suspects are highlighted, long marches and candle light vigils are announced. As citizens take on a much delayed ownership of the sea and reminisce their personal association with the culture around it, it is soon clear that after the initial dismay and efforts to protest, life eases back to its everyday routine. This acceptance is not far from the reality of our own lives where every encroachment is initially received with expressions of alarm and then gradually welcomed as an everyday norm. Poignantly Samundar ki Chori reminds us of the fate we deservedly face as our city and natural resources slowly disappear from our lives.

    Zambeel Dramatic Readings aims to present texts in Urdu and English rendered in their dramatised form, to create a dynamic collusion between literature and performance. Referencing traditions of storytelling and the contemporary form of the radio play, our works traverse time and geographical boundaries to interpret and enliven narratives through sound and recitation.

    Projection Site : At the seaside, Seaview, behind Dolmen Mall.

  • Dispatches from the Edge/
    End of the World

    Mehreen Murtaza

    15 February 2015

    Dispatches from the Edge/End of the World - Mehreen Murtaza

    Dispatches from the Edge/ End of the World was projected in what appeared to be an oasis of landfill that lay hidden underneath the network of Lyari Expressway’s smoothly constructed concrete and asphalt structures.  The monumental highway barriers form a periphery around the makeshift neighborhood.

    Families from various ethnicities that were forcefully evicted because of the construction of this highway now nestle themselves in makeshift homes dotted along the skinny Lyari river. The supine angles of the concrete monsters that cut across the night sky meet mines of garbage sourced from the entire city, laying claim to this dystopic landscape as No Man’s Land.

    Dispatches from the Edge/ End of the World is a jilted narrative of a lonesome plastic palm tree against the urban landscape of a what appears to be a ubiquitous construction site. Like psychograms transferred to a physical state, the clicks, hisses, gurgling and screeching voices that accompany the video metamorphose into decelerated moving images.

    Dispatches from the Edge/ End of the World explores the notion of progress and technology as a time-warping device, offering in the present time an event, experience or object that belongs to the past. The idea of the ghost (something present in absence) haunts, and obsolete (or ‘retro’) technology is employed to represent this rupture in time.

    Projection Site : Lyari Express Way

  • To know in every distortion of the light what fracture is

    Zahra Malkani

    1 March 2015
    To know in every distortion of the light what fracture is
    - Zahra Malkani

    Images of individual missing persons projected at the sites from which they were disappeared. Research and images for this series of projections were derived from various online databases and archives documenting state enforced disappearances specifically in relation to the Baloch community.In the struggle to recover missing persons and uncover the circumstances of their disappearance, the photograph of a missing person becomes key evidence as a 'certificate of presence' [1]. These images circulate widely in various forms across various dimensions; as posters in press club protests, framed photographs carried on foot from Quetta to Islamabad, MMS attachments circulating through cell phones, 10th generation jpegs, cropped and pixelated, screenshots and thumbnails, stored and shared on social media, blogs, websites and online archives; the degrading visual surface of each image bearing traces, a second layer of evidence, of the conditions of its production and circulation. Visible, in its incoherence, the struggle of the image to be preserved, to persevere. Evidence of the struggle, the refusal to disappear, to be disappeared.

    Mapping these moments of disappearance back onto the city, these projections aim to create ephemeral memorial landscapes to the missing at the site of the disappearance. To see, in fleeting moments, the city through its absences. [1] Roland Barthes, Camera Lucida.

    Projection Site : Civil Hospital, ISM Hospital & Gul Plaza, Saddar.

  • Ephemeral projections of a beached ship becoming

    Yaminay Chaudhri

    7 March 2015
    Ephemeral projections of a beached ship becoming
    -Yaminay Chaudhri

    Ephemeral projections of a beached ship becoming. Its hazy image reaches a gridded façade under construction, grazes the uneven surface of decaying masonry, re-appears as a faint specter in the real estate mundi of Bahria Town Properties. Submerged by speculating dealers. Files, forms, foam, dust, near-prime investment options... 

    A series of 7 minute video projections in and around the Bahria 'Icon' tower construction site, Clifton, Karachi navigate through the speculative and aspirational landscape of big development in South Asian cities. The name, Bahria, which translates to ‘Navy’ in Urdu, raises a question about the complicity between military interest and real estate development in Pakistan.

    The appearance of a stranded ship on the shores of Karachi is timed perfectly to the specter of this rising construction project, the tallest building to be built in the city. A precarious symbol of expectation; a ship out of water becomes a metaphor for various scales of longing and aspiration.

    Projection Sites : On the west glass facade of Bahria 'Icon' tower, on its adjacent construction site and new boundary wall enclosing Abdullah Shah Ghazi mausoleum, and in the Bahria Town real estate Mundi on Tauheed commercial area (a site of daily-and hourly- trading of Bahria town files, forms, and real estate options).

  • Teesri Manzil Kay Badaltay Nazaaray

    Fazal Rizvi

    22 April 2015
    Teesri Manzil Kay Badaltay Nazaaray - Fazal Rizvi

    'That view from my old house, once showed a mountain Now that view or that mountain has a crack.'

    Rizvi grew up in a two story house and remembers looking at a mountain in the distant from the roof on the 3rd floor. However he does not remember when and why that mountain was cut into two.Drawing upon visible geographies of the city, Rizvi imagines and writes narratives around these manipulated sites hinting at the politics, tensions, despair and life that surrounds them.

    Projection Site : Near Punjab Chowrangi
