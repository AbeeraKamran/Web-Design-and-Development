Afshin Dehkordi (b. 1975) is a socially-engaged visual artist. In his current work, Abu Omar is Waiting For You, he is working with psychiatrists and young asylum seekers, investigating trauma and migration through printmaking.

He is also publishing his first book, 'Breakline Verse”, the conclusion of a two year project combining pedagogical imagery with a photographic archive created by twelve disadvantaged teenagers in Tehran, Iran.

He co-founded studioSTRIKE, a south London arts space and studios, hosting 18 artists and a community cinema. He established and curated the Arts Council funded Bread and Roses Festival incorporating performance artists, photographic exhibitions, film screenings, artist talks and workshops. The festival was community based, involving a number of local grassroots community groups and all events were free to attend.

He combines his art practice with visiting lecturing posts in Photography and Film. He has taught at universities in the UK and abroad and received funding by the UK Government to teach postgraduate students at India’s National Institute of Design.

Before his art practice, Afshin worked as an award-winning BBC journalist for eight years and studied Mathematics at UCL. He was one of 12 Associate Artists selected for the year-long, Barbican-funded pioneering arts residency/alternative postgraduate arts school, Open School East.

Saeed Taji Farouky(b. 1978) is a Palestinian-British artist and filmmaker who has been producing work around themes of conflict, human rights and colonialism since 1998.

In 2011 he was awarded a TED Senior Fellowship for his work, and was previously named Artist-in-Residence at Tate Britain and the British Museum.

His latest film, Tell Spring Not to Come This Year, premiered at the Berlinale 2015 where it won the Audience Choice Award for Panorama documentary, and the Amnesty International human rights film award. The Guardian gave the film a 4-star review, calling it “a powerful and beautifully shot documentary.”

In 2016, he was selected for the artists moving image development programme The Artists Cut where his work was broadcast on Channel 4. He has been a human rights educator with Amnesty International since 2006.

Carl Fraser received his PhD from the University of Sheffield Architectural Department in 2016. His PhD explores the relationship between citizens and their representatives and how that relationship plays out in publicly accessible environments. The thesis focuses on London and physical spaces of non-commercial exchange within neoliberal society.

Carl completed his RIBA parts I and II at the Architectural Association in 2007 and has since worked in a number of small architectural practices in London while tutoring architecture at several universities in England from first year to masters level.

He is a member of the White Rose Society and has been a contributing speaker at the Theoretical Currents II conference series (Lincoln University, 2012) and the Text Cities series (University of Hertfordshire, 2015). He has also been on organiser and contributor in the Common Grounds (II) Series (2012), the Lines of Flight series (University of Sheffield, 2012) and the Mapping Agencies International Workshop (Faculté d’Architecture de l’ULB, 2013) as well as an invited speaker at the Contested Spaces round-table discussion (Central St. Martins - UAL, London Festival of Architecture, 2015)

He is Honorary Senior Lecturer in Child and Adolescent Psychiatry at Imperial College, London, and has carried out numerous research studies regarding young refugees and published extensively on the topic. Dr. Hodes also works as a consultant Child and Adolescent Psychiatrist in the NHS with CNWL NHS Foundation Trust.

Dr. Kamran Saedi is also a special scientific advisor to the project. He is a Fellow of the Royal College of Psychiatrists, with over 35 years professional experience working with refugees. He is currently working with unaccompanied asylum seeking and refugee young people through the charity he founded, Hope for the Young.
